An approaching dream ... a promising cure" for most types of cancer

Humanity’s ultimate dream in the battle of cancer may be to find one successful treatment in dealing with all types of cancer, a dream that seems to be on a date with a big step on the road to achieving it, according to a study published in Nature Immunology. A team of researchers at Cardiff University was able to discover a type of killer T immune cell that has a new type of receptor that can identify and kill most types of cancer cells without harming healthy cells, which opens new horizons for treating many types of cancer.
Anti-cancer T-cell therapies - which include removing and modifying immune cells and returning them to a patient's blood to search for and destroy cancer cells - are the latest model in advanced cancer treatments.
The most widely used type is known as CAR-T, and it is specific to each patient, but it targets a limited number of cancers, and has not been successful in treating the solid tumors that make up the majority of cancers. Conventional T cells scan the surface of other cells, to find what appears anomalous to eliminate cancer cells, while ignoring cells that only contain normal proteins, and the scan identifies small parts of cellular proteins associated with cell surface molecules, called Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA), which allows killer T cells to see what is happening inside cells by scanning their surface.
The new type recognizes a molecule called MR1 that is present on the surface of a wide range of cancer cells and normal cells, and is able to distinguish between healthy and cancerous cells, by killing only the latter, which qualifies it to be a target for more immunotherapy treatments.
The researchers discovered that this type of T cells found in the blood can recognize and destroy many types of cancer cells and attack many types of cancer, and the idea of ​​the new study is to take a sample from the blood of a cancer patient, then the T cells are extracted from the sample, modified genetically and reprogrammed from In order to acquire a receptor capable of detecting cancer, then the modified cells are grown in ample numbers in the laboratory before being re-injected into the patient. It is the same method used in the CAR-T program, which is a cancer immunotherapy that includes genetic modification of T cells to stimulate the immune system to recognize and destroy specific cancers.
